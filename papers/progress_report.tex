\begin{abstract}
\noindent This report aims to provide a summary of the progress made so far in our Capstone project, an interactive dashboard designed to forecast US equity sector performance (XLK, XLE, XLF, XLV, XLI) using data between 2005 and 2026. Data acquisition and preprocessing have been completed, including the integration of Stooq financial data, Federal Funds rates, and manually curated macroeconomic/geopolitical events. Through Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA), we have identified heterogeneous sector responses to monetary policy cycles as well as to global events such as the COVID-19 pandemic. ARIMA, GARCH, and LSTM models have been developed to forecast sector performance, showing promising results, including ARIMA currently outperforming LSTM models for all sectors at present. We have encountered noisy financial time series data and unreliable event data through Wikidata APIs which have been challenging to overcome. To mitigate these problems, we have used longer volatility windows, applied dropout regularization, and a curated event cache. Currently, we are in the development phase of building an interactive dashboard to display model outputs, including future developments such as event overlays and real-time responsiveness.
\end{abstract}

\section{Background}


\subsection{Changes and Literature Review Insights}
We have refined our event database to highlight those events related to 'Monetary' and 'Geopolitical' events, as there was no inherent measure of event significance. This allowed us to manually select a limited amount of significant macro events of interest given their small numbers.
Our reading of Ewing et al. (2003) and Ewing (2002) served as a source of motivation for our chosen variables and methodological extensions. For instance, Ewing et al. (2003) demonstrate that unanticipated monetary policy tightening, as identified through their measure of shocks to the Federal Funds Rate, has statistically significant negative impulse response effects on Financials and Capital Goods sectors. Although their study does not examine correlations, it nonetheless informed our choice to use a proxy measure of Fed Funds as a key driver of monetary policy. Furthermore, generalized forecast error variance decompositions presented in Ewing (2002) demonstrate that Industrials often explain a large fraction of the variation in other sectors' returns. This measure of shock transmission, as opposed to co-movement, nonetheless informed our choice to include a cross-sector correlation component to visualize potential channels of propagation within the broader equity markets.

\subsection{Alignment with Course Objectives}
The project continues to serve as a comprehensive application of the DATA 501 curriculum. By implementing a hybrid modeling approach (ARIMA vs. LSTM), we are directly addressing the course's emphasis on comparing classical statistical methods with modern machine learning. The integration of market data from Stooq, macroeconomic indicators from FRED, and a manually curated event metadata CSV exercises high-level data engineering skills. Broadly, this work mimics real-world financial analytics pipelines where data communication through interactive tools is as vital as the underlying predictive accuracy.

\section{Research Questions}

\subsection{Updates on Research Questions}
Our research questions are changing from qualitative comparisons to quantitative analysis. For example, research question 4, “Predictive accuracy of LSTM compared with ARIMA,” is now being addressed by calculating the RMSE/MAE for different time windows. Statistical significance tests are being conducted to ascertain whether the improvement in prediction by the LSTM justifies the increased computation required by the models. The visualizations are helping the variance analysis significantly and are bringing us closer to answering our research questions by helping identify trends and patterns.

\subsection{Modifications to the Original Questions}
We have moved beyond our original "all events" focus to a categorical approach. We have refined Question 2 by breaking out \textit{monetary shocks} (FOMC rate increases) from \textit{geopolitical shocks}. This allows us to separate "Fed-sensitive" sectors (XLK, XLF) from "Energy-sensitive" sectors (XLE). We also added a sub-question about \textit{asymmetric volatility}, or whether sectors recover as quickly as they fall in response to negative events.

\subsection{New Emerging Trends}
Preliminary results suggest that the "Technology" (XLK) sector has transitioned from being primarily growth-oriented to a more interest-rate-sensitive "utility-like" behavior during the post-pandemic era. We also observe that cross-sector correlations have hit a multi-year high in 2024, possibly indicating a "regime of synchronization" that limits the benefits of sector-based diversification.

\section{Methodology and Implementation}

\subsection{Dataset Selection}
Our main source is daily OHLCV time series for five sector ETFs (XLK, XLE, XLF, XLV, XLI) for the period from February 2005 to March 2026. We also include the Federal Funds Effective Rate series from FRED and a custom \texttt{events.csv} with important macroeconomic and geopolitical event dates (Lehman Brothers Bankruptcy, US Credit Rating Downgrade, Start of Oil Price Collapse, Brexit Referendum, COVID-19 Pandemic Declaration).

\subsection{Data Cleaning and Pre-processing}
To prepare the data for time-series modeling, we implemented:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Frequency Alignment:} All datasets were aligned to a standard business day frequency ('B'), with missing holiday values handled through forward-filling.
    \item \textbf{Normalization:} We computed daily log returns ($r_t = \ln(P_t / P_{t-1})$) to achieve stationarity.
    \item \textbf{Volatility Metrics:} Annualized rolling standard deviation (30-day window) was calculated for all sectors.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)}
For our EDA, we decided to focus on "visualizing 'Shock Windows.'" We utilized "Plotly" to place vertical markers for manually curated macro events on price charts for each sector. An important aspect of our EDA is \textbf{Event Response Drill-Down}, where cumulative log returns are calculated over a 30-day (or approximately 22 trading day) window following each event.

\subsection{Visualization Development}
The visualization layer is built using \texttt{Plotly}. So far, there are various implementations, such as normalized price trend charts, 30-day rolling volatility charts, macro events placed on individual price charts for each sector, 6-month rolling correlation line charts with FEDFUNDS, and a faceted bar chart showing heterogeneous 30-day cumulative returns for each event across all sectors. A \texttt{Dash} interactive dashboard is the intended output.

\subsection{Tools and Techniques}
We use \texttt{statsmodels} for ARIMA, \texttt{arch} for GARCH, and \texttt{TensorFlow/Keras} for LSTM architectures, utilizing a many-to-one sequence-to-vector approach.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
For model performance evaluation, we use both RMSE and MAE. We divide the test period into two sub-periods: "Stable" and "Shock," where "Shock" days are those where the sector's rolling 30-day annualized volatility is above the 90th percentile of its test period distribution. We report the RMSE in two regimes.
\section{Results and Discussion}

\subsection{Initial Insights, Patterns, or Trends Discovered}
Our initial analysis confirms that sector sensitivity to monetary policy is indeed non-uniform. The rolling correlation of sector log returns with the Federal Funds Rate for the last 6 months indicates that sectors respond in different ways at different times. The rolling correlations also indicate that the response of the Energy sector represented by XLE is notably different compared to the response of the other sectors during the recent period of monetary policy tightening in 2022-2023. The modeling results for the period covering 546 test observations from 2024-01-01 to 2026-02-02 indicate that XLE and XLK have the highest prediction errors in the Shock regime compared to the other sectors. XLV is the most predictable sector in our analysis, with the lowest prediction errors in the Stable regime ($0.008$) and in the Shock regime ($0.016$). The Shock regime errors are $2 \text{--}3$ times larger compared to the Stable regime errors for all sectors. This confirms that macroeconomic shocks indeed increase the difficulty of sectoral return predictions.

\subsection{Unexpected Findings or Deviations from Hypotheses}
We initially hypothesized that LSTM models would outperform ARIMA(1,0,1) across all periods. Instead, ARIMA outperforms the LSTM in every sector and both regimes (see Table~\ref{tab:regime_rmse}). The gap is most pronounced for XLE, where the LSTM Stable RMSE (0.01543) is 37\% higher than ARIMA's (0.01125). We attribute this to the LSTM being trained for only 5 epochs, meaning the network likely has not converged. Increasing training epochs or adding early stopping based on validation loss are the most immediate levers for improvement.

\section{Challenges and Resolutions}

\subsection{Key Obstacles Encountered}
The biggest challenge faced in this case was the \textit{noise-to-signal ratio} in financial data. The initial models were picking up seasonal noise instead of financial signals. In addition, there were issues with redundant and/or irrelevant data from the Wikidata SPARQL endpoint that required curation.

\subsection{The Significance of Findings So Far}
Our findings show that a simple ARIMA(1,0,1) baseline currently outperforms the LSTM in all sectors and both market regimes. This underscores that deep learning models require adequate training to be competitive. Furthermore, the consistently higher Shock-regime RMSE across all models and sectors (roughly $2\text{--}3\times$ Stable-regime error) confirms that macroeconomic volatility is the primary driver of forecast error, independent of model type.


\subsection{Areas Requiring Further Work}
The area that requires further work is optimizing the user experience for the dashboard. The challenge is to present the high-dimensional data in an "intuitive drill down" manner. Another area for further work is to refine the design of the LSTM architecture in order to incorporate the "Event Category" as a categorical feature in an optimal manner.
